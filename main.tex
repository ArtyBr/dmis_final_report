\section*{Abstract}
Driving accidents claim over 1,600 lives annually in the UK and are the leading cause of avoidable death in young people, with the majority caused by behavioural errors. Identifying and assessing these errors in context is therefore critical, yet no current system directly correlates the driver's actions with the vehicle's behaviour and road environment to assess whether the driver responded appropriately to the driving situation. This project addresses that gap by developing a multi-modal system that synchronises road-facing and driver-facing video to identify specific driving mistakes, focusing on the case of failing to check mirrors before manoeuvres. The system combines deep learning-based lane detection; a visual odometry pipeline to detect turns; and head pose estimation to determine gaze direction. A rule-based Safety Monitor cross-references detected manoeuvres against the driver's gaze history to classify each event as safe or unsafe. The pipeline is deployed through a web application featuring job submission through an online GPU cluster and an interactive results display. 

Evaluated on 104 labelled manoeuvre events across approximately 350 video clips, the system achieved an F1 score of 0.81 for event detection and 92\% classification accuracy on matched events, comparing favourably with Zekany et al. on three of four manoeuvre types, noting that the evaluations differ in dataset and labelling conditions. A live deployment with six external users over four weeks produced 170 events, confirming that the system identifies mistakes users were not aware of.

\section{Introduction}

Driving remains one of the most dangerous routine activities in the UK, where around five people are killed and 80 injured every day due to driving-related incidents \cite{govuk_casualties_2024}. The vast majority of driving accidents occur due to driver error - most often stemming not from a fundamental inability to operate the vehicle, but from behavioural mistakes, namely lack of awareness \cite{road_accident_data, dia_collisions}.

Driver Monitoring Systems (DMS) are a technology aimed at tracking driver behaviour using a variety of sensors, including in-cabin video footage. They have recently been designated as an essential safety component in modern vehicles by EU regulation \cite{eurlex_2019_2144}. Most current DMS’ are proprietary - either made by traditional car companies (Toyota \cite{toyota_tmate}, JLR \cite{jlr_dms_news}) or by companies selling hardware/software packages (SmartEye \cite{smarteye_ai}, Nauto \cite{nauto_platform}). While some open-source projects, such as OpenPilot \cite{commaai_openpilot} and ZenRoad \cite{zenroad_app}, also exist, both proprietary and open-source solutions focus on detecting general distraction (such as looking at a phone or drowsiness) or tracking vehicle movement, rather than identifying specific driving mistakes (Figure \ref{fig:existing_tools}). No current system correlates what the driver is doing with what the vehicle is doing in order to determine whether the driver's actions were appropriate for the manoeuvre being performed.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
    \subfigure[SmartEye: proprietary in-cabin DMS.]{\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{research_images/smarteye_landing.png}}
    \subfigure[ZenRoad: open-source smartphone telematics.]{\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{research_images/zenroad_tracking_landing.png}}
    \caption{Examples of existing driver monitoring tools. Sources: \cite{smarteye_ai}, \cite{zenroad_app}.}
    \label{fig:existing_tools}
\end{figure}

The central idea behind this project is that a truly comprehensive understanding of driver behaviour cannot be determined solely from observing either the driver or the car’s movement in isolation. Instead, our objective was to implement a multi-modal approach, synchronising the driver’s state with the external road environment and the movement of the vehicle. As such, we developed a system capable of identifying driving mistakes, namely failure to check mirrors before manoeuvres, by processing synchronised dash-cam footage and in-cabin footage. The system leverages computer vision techniques, including head pose estimation from in-cabin footage to establish driver gaze direction, and neural networks combined with visual odometry from dash-cam footage to identify car manoeuvres such as lane changes and turns.

A system capable of objectively identifying driver errors has numerous potential applications, several of which are already pursued by existing DMS providers:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A feedback tool for learner drivers.
    \item Improving usage-based car insurance analytics, as demonstrated by Nauto's fleet insurance platform \cite{nauto_insurance}.
    \item In-car accident prevention and alert systems, such as those integrated into Toyota's T-Mate \cite{toyota_tmate} and JLR's DMS \cite{jlr_dms_news}.
\end{itemize}

In order to establish a clear goal and evaluation criteria, we focused on the learner driver use-case. While numerous apps exist to help learner drivers prepare for the theory test, no application currently assists with the practical side of driving by providing objective feedback on real driving behaviour. This project therefore represents an entirely new category of tool for learner driver support. The resulting system is a web application for post-hoc analysis, processing synchronised dash-cam and in-cabin footage to identify and timestamp specific driving errors. The application aims to improve the quality of driving sessions with friends and family, who are less likely to spot subtle mistakes like mirror-checks \cite{2nd2nonedrivingschoolLearningDrive}. The system's performance was evaluated by addressing the research question: `Can a multi-modal system combining road-facing and driver-facing video detect driving mistakes at an accuracy of at least 80\%?' To this end, the system's detection accuracy was measured against manually labelled ground truth data and compared against the closest existing work in the literature. In addition to this, we conducted a user study in order to establish the system's generalisability and ability to distinguish dangerous manoeuvres from safe ones.

\section{Background}
This section reviews the key computer vision techniques used in the project, covering lane detection, temporal tracking, gaze estimation and motion estimation as the four core components of the proposed system.

\subsection{Lane Detection}
Lane detection is a well-researched topic within computer vision (CV). It is an essential tool for many modern vehicle features, from lane assist to full self-driving systems. Lane detection aims to detect lane lines or lane areas by either camera or lidar \cite{tang_review_2021}. This project focuses on camera-based perception, due to the cost and scope constraints of using lidar. Classical lane detection methods involved image processing techniques such as Sobel \cite{sobel_1968} and Canny \cite{canny_1986} filters, followed by Hough transform \cite{duda_1972} and curve fitting. However, these methods suffered from poor performance and consistency in varying lighting conditions and faded or obscured lane lines \cite{hillel_2014}. Since the project aims to work on urban roads in different lighting and weather conditions, these methods are not suitable. 

The modern development of lane detection systems focuses on deep learning to create more robust and adaptable models, specifically using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). CNNs are an advancement of traditional Neural Networks (NNs), with convolutional and pooling layers added to the beginning of the architecture \cite{lecun_1998}, generalising NNs to work on images (Figure \ref{fig:cnn_vis}). These layers use convolutions \cite{goodfellow_2016} to extract relevant features from the input, which are then flattened and passed to traditional, fully connected layers for classification.

\begin{figure}[H]

\centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{research_images/cnn_explanation.png}
    \caption{Visualisation of CNN architecture. Source: \cite{mathworks_cnn}.}
    \label{fig:cnn_vis}

\end{figure}

Existing deep learning-based lane detection methods can be grouped into two categories: two-step and one-step methods \cite{tang_review_2021}. Two-step methods first perform feature extraction using heuristic recognition-based methods and deep learning, followed by post-processing with clustering and fitting. An example of this is LaneNet \cite{wang_lanenet_2018}, which generates a binary segmentation map and per-pixel embeddings, requiring a subsequent clustering step to distinguish individual lane instances. On the other hand, one-step methods can get the detection and clustering results directly from the image. Inspired by single-stage object detectors such as YOLO \cite{redmon_you_2016}, architectures like PolyLaneNet \cite{tabelini_polylanenet_2020} obtain lane curve parameters directly, bypassing the need for heuristic post-processing.

Table \ref{tab:lane_model_comparison} details the performance results of several state-of-the-art models on the two primary benchmarks: TuSimple \cite{TuSimple2017} and CULane \cite{CULane2018}. The data for TuSimple is limited to US highways and intersections (Figure \ref{fig:dataset_examples}a), while CULane features a range of environments, including urban streets and rural roads, and was collected under more challenging weather conditions such as rain and fog (Figure \ref{fig:dataset_examples}b). For this reason, the CULane benchmark is the more relevant one to our project.

\subsection{Temporal Tracking}
Standard lane detection models process each frame independently, producing a fresh set of detections with no awareness of previous frames. This lack of temporal continuity means that lane identity and position must be re-established every frame, which is insufficient for the project's requirement to track lane changes over time. Lane tracking addresses this by maintaining persistent lane state across frames, estimating the position and movement of lanes in time-series driving data. 

State-of-the-art research on this subject focuses on directly training NNs, such as Transformers \cite{vaswani2017attention}, with video data as input (rather than images). Models such as RVLD (Recursive Video Lane Detection) \cite{jin_recursive_2023}, which propagates the state of the current frame to subsequent frames, show high-performing results. Another modern approach, used by YOLOP \cite{wu_yolop_2022} and Openpilot \cite{chen_level_2022}, is to train an all-in-one model, which learns lane detection and tracking as well as movement/position estimation, road surface segmentation and object detection all at once. While such methods are currently at the academic frontier, the industry standard remains focused on more classical methods, such as the Kalman Filter. Recent reviews \cite{yurtsever2020survey} confirm that for safety-critical ADAS, separating `perception' (deep learning) from `state estimation' (Kalman filtering) is preferred due to its explainability, whereas the former methods act as a black box once trained. For this reason, the Kalman Filter approach was chosen for the project. 

The Kalman Filter is a temporal method which works in two phases: prediction and update (Figure \ref{fig:kalman_vis}). The filter stores the last known location and velocity of a lane in a ‘state vector’. It uses the current velocity to predict where the lane should be in the next frame. Next, it observes the actual detection from the model to update this prediction. If a lane is obscured or faded, the filter uses the prediction to `remember' the lane. This makes the system robust to per-frame fluctuations and lighting anomalies, ensuring consistency even when detections are missed.

\subsection{Gaze Estimation}

Gaze estimation is a process used to determine the direction of a person's head. This is achieved through face analysis, which stores the facial orientation and pose. Modern systems, such as Drive Sense \cite{salma_drive_2024}, typically use landmark detectors to extract key points (mouth, eyes, nose) from an image. To establish the angle of the 2D points, the Perspective-n-Point (PnP) algorithm is commonly used. PnP calculates the matrix required to transform a `canonical' 3D face shape to the detected location of the tracked points (Figure \ref{fig:pnpvis}). The matrix consists of the $3\times3$ rotation matrix $R$, followed by a $3\times1$ translation vector $t$, allowing the angles (yaw, pitch, roll) to be calculated.

\begin{figure}[H]

\centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{research_images/pnp.png}
    \caption{Visualisation of the PnP problem. Source: \cite{opencv_pnp}.}
    \label{fig:pnpvis}

\end{figure}

Once angles are extracted, they must be classified into discrete gaze directions (e.g. mirrors or forward). Dimensionality reduction techniques, such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) \cite{jolliffe2016principal}, are often used to transform the data, for example, from three angles to two, to make clusters of points corresponding to each gaze classification more well-defined.

For the final classification step, a choice exists between unsupervised clustering (e.g. K-Means \cite{macqueen1967some}) and supervised learning (e.g. Support Vector Machines or SVM \cite{cortes1995support}). While K-Means can show the quality of clusters without labels, it often struggles with outliers or classes that occupy narrow areas of the feature space. SVMs are well-suited to this task for several reasons: they are effective with small training sets because they rely only on the support vectors near the decision boundary rather than the entire dataset \cite{cortes1995support}; they produce deterministic, interpretable decision boundaries that can be visualised and verified; and their explainability aligns with the same principle discussed in Section 2.2, where transparent models are preferred over black-box approaches for safety-related classification.

\subsection{Motion Estimation}
Motion estimation is an essential topic within robotics and autonomous vehicle research, with the aim of identifying the relative direction, speed and position of a camera as it moves through an environment. For this project, motion estimation is needed to detect left and right turns. Two relevant methods for this are Visual Odometry (VO) and Visual Simultaneous Localisation and Mapping (vSLAM) \cite{taketomi2017visual}. vSLAM constructs a persistent map of the environment, and can identify loop-closures to correct for drift accumulation. However, it is considerably more computationally expensive than VO and requires camera calibration to function correctly, which would be impractical for a general user study with varying camera properties. Since only local, relative motion is needed for turn detection, VO was selected as sufficient without the overhead of full environment mapping.

VO analyses sequential camera frames to estimate the camera's local motion incrementally. The process begins with keypoint detection, where algorithms such as Shi-Tomasi \cite{shi1994good} or ORB \cite{rublee2011orb} identify distinctive features in each frame. These keypoints are then tracked across consecutive frames using sparse Optical Flow, a technique that estimates the displacement of individual feature points between frames rather than computing motion for every pixel. The Lucas-Kanade method \cite{lucas1981iterative} is commonly used for this, as it is efficient and well-suited to tracking a sparse set of points. For this project, Shi-Tomasi was selected for keypoint detection, as it produces corners with strong gradients in two directions, which is exactly what Lucas-Kanade is designed to track. Alternatives such as FAST or ORB were considered, but these produce lower-quality corners and include binary descriptor computation that is unnecessary when using optical flow for matching rather than descriptor comparison.

\begin{figure}[H]

\centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{research_images/optical_flow_ex.png}
    \caption{Optical flow example on a road scene, showing estimated motion vectors. Source: \cite{mathworks_optical_flow}}
    \label{fig:optical_flow_ex}

\end{figure}

Once correspondences between keypoints are established, the Essential Matrix is computed from the matched point pairs. The Essential Matrix encodes the geometric relationship between two camera views, and can be decomposed into a rotation matrix $R$ and translation vector $t$ that describe how the camera has moved between frames. This is analogous to the PnP decomposition used in gaze estimation (Section 2.3), but operates on frame-to-frame correspondences rather than a canonical 3D model. Since this project is limited to a single dash-cam, the variant of VO used is monocular, which naturally faces scale ambiguity, meaning it can only obtain relative motion estimates, and the motion estimate is likely to drift over time. 

One approach for classifying manoeuvres from VO output, introduced by Zekany et al. \cite{zekany2022finding}, involves using a deep learning VO model to estimate the ego-vehicle trajectory and comparing a sliding window of this trajectory against a predefined library of reference manoeuvres using Dynamic Time Warping (DTW), a classical distance metric for time-series comparison. However, the use of a deep learning model for the VO step makes the trajectory estimation itself a black box. Since this project requires the detection of a small subset of manoeuvres, rather than classification of a broad range of complex ones, a simpler heuristic-based approach can be adopted instead, consistent with the preference for explainable methods discussed in Section 2.2. This would mean that each detection decision can be traced to interpretable thresholds.

\section{Objectives}

The background research established the techniques needed to detect lane positions, track them over time, estimate driver gaze, and measure vehicle motion. This section defines the functional and non-functional requirements that guided how these techniques were combined into the system.

\subsection*{Functional Requirements}

\subsubsection*{1. Core Video Analysis and Mistake Detection (Must-Have)}
Given that robust lane detection, temporal tracking, and gaze estimation methods are readily available, the primary objective was to integrate these into a unified pipeline that identifies driving mistakes from synchronised dual-camera footage.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FR1.1} Accept two synchronised video files as input: road-facing and cabin-facing.
    \item \textbf{FR1.2} Analyse the dash-cam feed to detect and keep track of lane markings.
    \item \textbf{FR1.3} Process the output of FR1.2 to detect lane change events by identifying when a tracked lane crosses the centre of the frame.
    \item \textbf{FR1.4} Classify the driver's gaze direction per frame as either:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Looking ahead.
        \item Looking at the left mirror.
        \item Looking at the right mirror.
        \item Looking at the rear-view mirror.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FR1.5} Combine the outputs of the manoeuvre detection and driver behaviour models using a rule-based algorithm to identify when manoeuvres are made without a prior mirror-check.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{2. Web Interface and User Feedback (Must-Have)}
To enable user testing with external participants, the system requires a web interface that accepts user videos and presents the results.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FR2.1} Provide a web interface for a user to upload video footage after their driving session.
    \item \textbf{FR2.2} Display the type and count of detected mistakes.
    \item \textbf{FR2.3} Present synchronised video playback that allows the user to navigate to detected mistake timestamps.
    \item \textbf{FR2.4} Implement a user authentication and account system with per-user access control over uploaded videos and analysis results.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{3. Mistake Detection Enhancements (Should-Have)}
The background research also identified visual odometry as a means of detecting vehicle motion, and highlighted that gaze classifier generalisation across users remains a challenge. These observations motivate the following extensions.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FR3.1} Manoeuvre detection extension - Enhance manoeuvre detection to include:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{FR3.1.1} Turns into side roads.
        \item \textbf{FR3.1.2} 3-point turns.
        \item \textbf{FR3.1.3} Reversing.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FR3.2} Gaze classification extension (I) - Detect blind-spot checks.
    \item \textbf{FR3.3} Gaze classification generalisation - Provide a per-user gaze calibration system integrated into the web application, allowing the gaze classifier to generalise to different drivers, camera positions and seating arrangements.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{4. Extensions (Could-Have)}
Additional features that would enhance the system, but considered beyond the scope of the core project.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FR4.1} Data input extension - Allow the use of a smartphone camera to record internal footage.
    \item \textbf{FR4.2} Gaze classification extension (II) - Incorporate eye-tracking to improve gaze detection accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Non-Functional Requirements}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NFR1} The core analysis script must be well-documented and capable of being executed via the command line.
    \item \textbf{NFR2} The user interface must be intuitive to navigate and provide mistake identification information clearly to the user.
    \item \textbf{NFR3} Data must be stored securely and anonymised, to comply with UK GDPR.
\end{itemize}

\section{Design and Development}

With the objectives defined, this section describes the implementation. The project was implemented using a modular architecture, separating code into distinct sub-modules (Figure \ref{fig:pipeline_overview}), corresponding to the objectives: Lane Change Detection (FR1.2/3), Turn Detection (FR3.1.1), Gaze Estimation (FR1.4) and Web Application (FR2). This left room to extend the project with features, specifically those detailed in the Should-Have requirements, and to improve individual features further without affecting the rest of the system.

We aggregate the results from each module in a Safety Monitor class, which maintains a buffer storing each detected event and gaze classification. This is cross-referenced when an event is detected, determining whether each manoeuvre is safe or unsafe.

\subsection{Resources and Tools}
Rather than training models from scratch, the project utilises pre-trained models and standard tools to perform the analysis. Python, due to its robust ecosystem of data processing libraries, is the language chosen for most state-of-the-art ML research, so we use it in this project. While there are two prominent Python libraries for ML and NN development, PyTorch \cite{paszke2019pytorch} and TensorFlow \cite{abadi2016tensorflow}, we are only able to use models implemented in the PyTorch library. This is because the available development GPU (RTX 5070Ti) operates using a new CUDA compatibility protocol, `Compute Capability 12.0’, which is not yet supported by TensorFlow.

\subsection{Lane Change Detection (FR1.2 \& FR1.3)}
Lane detection allows us to represent the position of the car relative to the road, which in turn informs us of the vehicle’s movement, and specifically any manoeuvres that it performs. While the model we chose to use in the end was LaneATT, the process to come to this conclusion came with several challenges.

In order to establish if benchmark models would generalise to British streets and consumer hardware (dash-cams), all models were qualitatively tested on our self-collected driving dataset. Figure \ref{fig:drive_examples} displays the output on a representative frame from our dataset of 3 of the models: PolyLaneNet \cite{tabelini_polylanenet_2020}, Ultra-Fast Lane Detection (UFLD) \cite{qin2020ultra} and LaneATT \cite{tabelini2021keep}. A quantitative benchmark was infeasible, as this would require extensive manual labelling, taking time away from feature development. LaneATT was found to perform the most accurately and reliably.

A key limitation identified was the generalisability of many models to consumer dash-cam hardware, with several models showing poor performance on the self-collected dataset. A possible explanation is over-fitting to the camera parameters used during dataset collection. When applying these models to footage from cameras with different fields of view, warped lenses or compression artefacts, detection accuracy degrades significantly (Figures \ref{fig:drive_examples}a, \ref{fig:drive_examples}b). The attention mechanism used by the LaneATT model may explain its increased robustness under these conditions, although this topic is not discussed in the literature. Specialising the models to our hardware would require manual labelling for re-training and would be impractical for a general user study, so the most generalisable model, LaneATT, was chosen for the project.

Another problem was that the datasets that are used for training models (TuSimple, CULane) contain only straight or slightly curved road examples. Because of this, roundabouts and roads with significant curvature fail to be detected. Additionally, there do not exist any specific datasets or research papers that investigate the specific case of driving on roundabouts. As such, roundabouts remain a limitation of the project.

Lane tracking was implemented by storing lane information in `LaneTrack’ objects, which use a Kalman filter to store and update their state. An overall `LaneTracker’ object is responsible for matching previously stored lanes to newly detected lanes. Initially, the Hungarian algorithm \cite{kuhn1955hungarian} was used for this matching process, however it always produces a complete matching even when distances between lanes are very large. This resulted in scenarios where the system matched lanes across the entire image if a lane on the left side of the image stopped being tracked, and a new lane was detected on the right side. 

Instead, we switched to a ‘closest first’ approach with a gate-based cut-off (Listing \ref{lst:lane-tracker-gated}). The first-closest pairs are matched to each other, and if a pair’s distance exceeds the cut-off point, they are not matched and the detected lane is stored as a new lane rather than updating an existing one, while the stored lane is counted as a miss. This change eliminated such scenarios and made the tracking work more reliably in practice (Figure \ref{fig:cars_lanes}).

\begin{lstlisting}[
    style=pythonstyle,
    caption={Closest-first gated data association for lane tracking (key logic)},
    label={lst:lane-tracker-gated}
]
class LaneTracker:
    def update(self, detections):
        # Predict existing tracks
        predictions = [t.predict() for t in self.tracks]

        # If no detections: count misses, prune stale tracks, then check lane-cross events
        if not detections:
            self._prune_tracks()
            self.cross_events = [
                {"track_id": t.id, "direction": d}
                for t in self.tracks
                if (d := t.check_lane_cross())
            ]
            return

        # Distance-based cost between predicted and detected lane polynomials
        C = self._calculate_cost_matrix(predictions, detections)

        # Closest-first matching with gating
        matches = []
        Cg = C.copy()
        while Cg.size > 0:
            r, c = np.unravel_index(np.argmin(Cg), Cg.shape)
            if C[r, c] > 300:  # gate threshold
                break
            matches.append((r, c))
            Cg[r, :] = np.inf
            Cg[:, c] = np.inf

        matched = set()
        for r, c in matches:
            self.tracks[r].update(detections[c])
            matched.add(c)

        # Unmatched detections initialise new tracks; unmatched tracks accumulate misses
        self._create_new_tracks([detections[i] for i in range(len(detections)) if i not in matched])
        self._prune_tracks()
\end{lstlisting}

Detected lane change events are stored in the \texttt{Safety Monitor} class' buffer.

\subsection{Turn Detection (FR3.1.1)}
Turn detection is necessary to extend mistake identification beyond lane changes to include failures to check mirrors before turning at junctions, a common fault on driving tests. Since the project does not have access to vehicle telemetry (steering angle, GPS waypoints), the only available signal is the road-facing video itself. This motivated the use of Visual Odometry (VO) to estimate the vehicle's heading change over time.

Two established open-source VO systems were evaluated: TartanVO \cite{wang2021tartanvo} and DeepV2D \cite{teed_deepv2d_2020}. TartanVO, a learning-based approach, generalised poorly to dash-cam footage, likely because its training data (TartanAir) consists of synthetic indoor and outdoor scenes with very different visual characteristics to UK road footage. DeepV2D suffered from dependency conflicts and, like TartanVO, operated as a monolithic system whose internal parameters were difficult to tune for the specific failure modes observed (e.g.\ drift during stops at traffic lights, erratic estimates when large vehicles fill the frame).

Because the project only requires yaw-based turn detection rather than metrically accurate 3D reconstruction, a custom lightweight VO pipeline was developed instead, as suggested by the external advisor at JLR. This offered full control over each processing stage, making it straightforward to diagnose and fix failure modes as they arose, an advantage that proved critical during iterative development. Existing functions provided by the OpenCV Python library made creating this pipeline straightforward to implement. The pipeline operates by extracting and matching visual features between consecutive frames, then estimating the relative camera motion from the resulting point correspondences. 

Frame-by-frame, we perform a centre crop (75\%) of the road video. The crop removes peripheral objects which otherwise introduced significant noise, such as radial lens distortion, stickers on the windscreen and the hood of the car. For each frame, a Mask R-CNN \cite{he2017mask} instance segmentation model identifies dynamic objects (cars, cyclists and pedestrians) and produces a binary mask. This masking step is essential because optical flow from moving vehicles would otherwise be interpreted as camera ego-motion, causing large errors in the estimated rotation. Early iterations without dynamic masking produced frequent false turn detections during surrounding vehicle movement while the recording car was stationary.

Features are detected only on the unmasked (static) regions using a grid-based bucketing strategy, which divides the image into a $4 \times 6$ grid and runs feature tracking independently in each cell. Without bucketing, detected features would be evenly spread out through the image, with many points detected in non-feature-rich areas of the image, such as repeating textures on housing tiles and roads (Figure \ref{fig:points_houses}). In such regions, visually similar patches across consecutive frames caused incorrect point correspondences, introducing substantial noise into the tracking algorithm. When the car was stationary or moving very slowly, the noise took over the tracking and caused extremely sharp, inaccurate pose predictions.

\begin{figure}[H]

\centering
    \subfigure[]{\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{driving_images/house_without_grid.png}}
    \subfigure[]{\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{driving_images/houses_with_grid.png}} 
    \caption{(a) Tracked features without bucketing: points are evenly spread out on road and brick textures, and would produce incorrect matchings in subsequent frames. (b) Tracked features with bucketing: Points cluster to feature-dense areas of the image, and stay tracked to those areas in subsequent frames.}
    \label{fig:points_houses}

\end{figure}

Features are tracked between consecutive frames using Lucas-Kanade optical flow. A forward-backward consistency check \cite{kalal2010forward} is applied. This means that points are tracked forward to the current frame, then backward to the previous frame, and only matches with a re-projection error below 0.5 pixels are retained. This eliminates poorly tracked points that would otherwise introduce noise into the pose estimate, particularly near the edges of the dynamic object mask, where partial occlusions by cars and people caused errors in tracking. The essential matrix is then estimated via RANSAC \cite{fischler1981random}, and the relative rotation and translation are recovered using \texttt{cv2.recoverPose}. One major improvement was to reject pose estimations between frames which are deemed physically implausible. A common example was if the calculated rotation exceeded an unrealistic threshold ($\sim$28\textdegree), which often occurred during slow-moving sequences due to still unaccounted-for sources of noise.

Raw pose estimates are accumulated and fused through a 12-state Kalman filter tracking position ($x, y, z$), orientation (roll, pitch, yaw) and their velocities. The Kalman filter serves two purposes: it smooths the noisy frame-to-frame VO estimates into a coherent trajectory, and it maintains a reasonable state estimate during periods where VO measurements are unreliable (Figure \ref{fig:kalman_comparison}). To achieve the latter, the measurement noise covariance is adjusted dynamically based on tracking confidence. When the vehicle is moving and tracking is successful, standard noise is applied. When the vehicle is stationary (low optical flow), higher confidence is placed on the prediction to prevent drift from noisy near-zero estimates. When the segmentation mask covers most of the frame (a ``blind'' condition occurring in heavy traffic), the filter almost entirely trusts its own prediction, as there are insufficient static features to produce a meaningful VO measurement.

\begin{figure}[H]

\centering
    \subfigure[]{\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{driving_images/tracking_without_kalman.png}}
    \subfigure[]{\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{driving_images/tracking_with_kalman.png}} 
    \caption{Tracking results from the same video clip: (a) without Kalman filter or implausible-frame rejection, showing implausible and noisy tracking while the vehicle is stationary before a turn; (b) with Kalman filter and frame rejection, with improved tracking performance.}
    \label{fig:kalman_comparison}

\end{figure}

Turn detection monitors the accumulated yaw rotation via the \texttt{TurnDetector} class. A turn event is triggered under one of two conditions: (1) the accumulated yaw exceeds 80\textdegree\ regardless of sharpness, to capture regular turns out of or into side-streets; or (2) the accumulated yaw exceeds 15\textdegree\ combined with a sudden spike ($>$8\textdegree\ in a single frame), catching aggressive but more subtle manoeuvres such as roundabout exits. A straightness reset clears the accumulator if rotation remains below 0.1\textdegree/frame for 30 consecutive frames ($\sim$1 second), preventing slow drift from eventually triggering a false detection. A 60-frame cooldown after each detection prevents duplicate events from the same turn. Detected turns are classified as \texttt{LEFT} or \texttt{RIGHT} and fed into the Safety Monitor, which validates them against the driver's gaze history using the same 5-second look-back window and mirror-check rules as lane changes.

\subsection{Face Analysis (FR1.4)}
Facial landmark extraction was implemented using the InsightFace \cite{deng2019arcface} library, which was selected due to its robustness under varying lighting conditions and large face rotations. Google’s MediaPipe \cite{lugaresi2019mediapipe} was also tested, however it frequently failed to maintain landmark tracking when the face rotated too far, making it unsuitable for this application. This is likely because MediaPipe is mainly used for front-facing phone camera applications such as Snapchat filters, whereas InsightFace was designed for face recognition and tracking from sources such as CCTV footage, requiring robust tracking under more challenging conditions. 

Using the landmarks extracted by InsightFace, head pose angles were calculated using the PnP algorithm. To classify discrete gaze directions, a sample video clip containing all required face directions was analysed (Figure \ref{fig:yawpitchroll_1}a). The yaw angle provided the most useful information for determining gaze direction, while pitch was found to be correlated with yaw due to the camera perspective. The estimated direction is visualised in Figure \ref{fig:gaze_examples} with a line coming from the nose.

The raw angle data has three dimensions (yaw, pitch, roll), but not all contribute equally to distinguishing gaze directions. PCA was applied to project the data onto its two principal components (Figure \ref{fig:yawpitchroll_1}b), removing the least informative axis and producing tighter, more separable clusters for each gaze direction. The resulting two-dimensional representation also makes the classification boundaries easier to visualise and interpret during calibration.

\subsection{Mistake Identification (FR1.5)}
The system identifies the driving mistake of failing to check mirrors prior to manoeuvres, including both lane changes and turns. When a manoeuvre is detected (either a lane crossing from the lane tracker or a turn event from the visual odometry module), the Safety Monitor checks the driver's gaze history over the previous 5 seconds. If the driver checked the corresponding mirror (left mirror for rightward manoeuvres, right mirror for leftward manoeuvres) within this window, the event is classified as safe; otherwise, it is classified as unsafe. A rear mirror check is also noted if present, but is not sufficient on its own.

A rule-based heuristic was chosen over a learned classifier because it produces interpretable and auditable decisions. The UK Highway Code states that drivers should check mirrors ``in good time'' before any change of direction or speed \cite{highwaycode2025}, but does not prescribe a precise threshold. A 5-second lookback window was chosen as a practical compromise: long enough to capture a check performed shortly before the manoeuvre begins, but short enough that the observed traffic state remains relevant. This was also inspired by the approach taken by Zekany et al.\ \cite{zekany2022finding}, who use a 4-second ``Maneuver Time Error'' (MTE) threshold in their evaluation of dashboard camera manoeuvre detection. Our slightly larger 5-second window allows for real-time driver reaction times while still ensuring mirror checks are performed close to the time of the actual manoeuvre (Figure \ref{fig:safe_lane_change}).

\subsection{Web Application (FR2)}
The web application was built to facilitate a user study and obtain general feedback and interest on such a system from a wide audience. Beyond the core computer vision pipeline, building a responsive and functional application required: facilitating the upload and synchronisation of large video files; deploying a backend which could take advantage of the GPUs on the shared university infrastructure; and displaying the results of the processing on a clear, intuitive interface. A particular difficulty was designing workflows for tasks that are inherently user-specific and cannot be fully automated, such as gaze calibration, where each user's seating position and camera angle produce different head pose distributions that must be individually labelled and trained. Another challenge was determining how to present the analysis results effectively; the interface needed to display detected events, gaze history, timeline annotations and calibration tools without overwhelming users who are not technically trained. 

Iterative user testing was conducted in order to find the right balance between giving users enough control to calibrate and verify their results while keeping the interface intuitive. The resulting application allows users to upload their footage, which is analysed and the results presented on an interactive display with synchronised video playback and navigation to detected events.

\subsubsection{Overall System and Backend Design}
The application is designed as a Client-Server-Worker system to handle the heavy video processing requirements while keeping the UI smooth and responsive (Figure \ref{fig:architecture}). A React-based frontend communicates with a Python-based FastAPI backend running on a separate port on the same host. Users upload video footage via \texttt{POST} requests and retrieve job status and analysis results via \texttt{GET} requests. The frontend and backend are separated into distinct servers so that static asset serving does not compete with API request handling, and both run behind TLS certificates to secure user data in transit. The university's DCS large storage provides centralised storage for uploaded videos, analysis results, and the SQLite application database, ensuring all components can access the same data without requiring file transfers between machines.

\subsubsection{User Authentication and Account System (FR2.4)}
The initial prototype operated without user accounts, meaning anyone with the URL could upload videos and view all results. This was acceptable during development but inadequate for real-world use, where analysis results contain sensitive footage and users need to manage their own history. A user account system was therefore added, with registration, login, and per-user access control over uploaded videos and results.

Authentication is handled via JSON Web Tokens (JWT) with a 7-day expiry limit, chosen over session-based authentication for its stateless nature, which avoids the need for server-side session storage. Account activation requires admin approval after registration, effectively acting as a whitelist that allows us to verify each user’s driver’s licence as required by the ethical approval board.

\subsubsection{Upload Flow and Video Synchronisation}
The frontend was built using React with Vite as the build tool and Bootstrap for styling. React’s component-based architecture suited the modular interface, with the video player, timeline, calibration editor and upload flow implemented as independent components sharing state through React’s context system. The upload flow supports the submission of multiple consecutive video files, which is necessary as dash-cam systems use loop-recording rather than saving entire driving sessions in a single file. Files are uploaded in segments and spliced server-side using FFmpeg’s concat demuxer, handling the common case of multiple sequential recordings transparently. This step also converts all videos to a common MP4 format, as during early user testing we found that some dash-cams record video in formats which were previously incompatible with the pipeline. 

\begin{figure}[H]

\centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{website_images/analysis_submission.png}
    \caption{Screenshot of the video submission page, showing video submission boxes for road-facing and cabin-facing video, as well as a checkbox for choosing whether to synchronise videos via audio.}
    \label{fig:video_submission}

\end{figure}

A key usability challenge was that users cannot be expected to manually align their two video streams before uploading. Most users record with separate devices (e.g.\ a dedicated dash-cam and a phone mounted on the dashboard), which start at different times and have no shared clock signal. Requiring users to determine the offset themselves using external video editing tools would make the application impractical for the target audience of learner drivers and their supervisors. The system therefore needed an automated synchronisation step that handles alignment transparently, while still giving users the ability to verify and correct the result when the automatic method is uncertain.

The synchronisation algorithm uses the fact that both cameras record video within the same audio environment. Engine noise, speech, indicator clicks and road noise are captured by both microphones, providing a reference signal for alignment. The process begins by extracting mono PCM audio from both videos using FFmpeg, downsampled to 4000\,Hz which is sufficient for detecting the offset while keeping computation fast. The extracted signals are normalised by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation, removing differences in microphone sensitivity and recording volume.

The temporal offset is then computed using FFT-based cross-correlation via SciPy’s \texttt{fftconvolve} function. Cross-correlation measures the similarity between two signals as a function of a time lag applied to one of them. By computing this in the frequency domain using the Fast Fourier Transform, the operation runs efficiently even on long audio signals. The lag corresponding to the peak of the cross-correlation function gives the sample offset between the two recordings, which is converted to seconds using the known sample rate. A confidence score is derived by normalising the peak correlation value relative to the total signal energy, producing a value between 0 and 1 that indicates how reliable the computed offset is.

The synchronisation result is presented to the user through a confirmation interface before analysis begins (Figure \ref{fig:audio_sync}). The interface displays both audio waveforms on a draggable timeline, with a visual indicator showing the computed alignment point. If the user notices an error in the results, they can manually adjust the offset using fine-grained controls ($\pm$0.1\,s and $\pm$0.01\,s increments) or by dragging the waveforms directly. A synchronised preview playback allows the user to verify the alignment visually and by playing the audio of each video before confirming. Once confirmed, the synchronised videos are submitted for analysis and the user is shown progress updates until processing completes.

\subsubsection{Per-User Gaze Calibration}
The default SVM gaze classifier was trained on head pose data collected from a single individual in a specific vehicle with a specific camera position. During user testing, it became clear that this model did not generalise well: differences in seating position, camera angle, head size and facial geometry meant that the decision boundaries trained on one person’s data frequently misclassified another’s gaze directions (Figure \ref{fig:uncalib_timeline} shows uncalibrated gaze classifications, while Figure \ref{fig:calib_timeline} displays the timeline after calibration). Since the gaze classifier is the foundation of all safety judgements, poor classification accuracy renders the entire system unreliable. This motivated the development of a per-user calibration system as an extension.

The initial calibration interface provided a clip editor (Figure \ref{fig:calib_editors}a), where users mark time segments in their previously analysed videos and label them with the corresponding gaze direction (e.g.\ ``Left Mirror’’, ``Looking Ahead’’). The system extracts the stored head pose angles (yaw, pitch, roll) from the existing analysis results for the marked segments, avoiding the need to re-run face detection. Once sufficient samples are collected (a minimum of 3 per mandatory class: Left Mirror, Right Mirror, Looking Ahead; with Rear Mirror optional since not all vehicles have a visible interior rear-view mirror), the user triggers model training, which fits a new PCA transformer and SVM classifier on their personal data. Diagnostic plots (head pose scatter, PCA projection, and SVM decision boundary) are generated and displayed so the user can visually verify that the class regions correspond to their actual gaze directions before using the model.

During early user testing, several participants found the clip editor daunting, as it required them to scrub through their videos to find segments where they were looking at each mirror and then mark precise time boundaries. This process typically took around five minutes of trial and error before users felt confident in their calibration. To address this, a point editor (Figure \ref{fig:calib_editors}b) was added, allowing users to directly view, add, or remove individual training points on the PCA scatter plot rather than working through video clips. This reduced the calibration process to under a minute for most users, since they could simply correct the few misclassified points rather than re-selecting entire video segments. Some users still found the clip editor useful when they had deliberately looked at each mirror at the start of a recording for the purpose of calibration, so both interfaces were retained. The interface also supports ``Save As’’ (duplicating a calibration to experiment with different training sets without losing the original) and renaming.

\subsubsection{Privacy and Video Output Processing}
The driver-facing camera inevitably captures passengers and, depending on the camera’s field of view, potentially pedestrians or other bystanders visible through windows. Distributing or displaying this footage without consent raises ethical and legal concerns, particularly under GDPR. Since the system’s analysis only requires the driver’s face, all other faces can be obscured without any loss of functionality.

During analysis, the \texttt{FaceAnalyzer} identifies the driver using a heuristic: in landscape-oriented footage (the typical dash-cam configuration), the driver is the largest face on the left side of the frame (corresponding to the right-hand-drive UK vehicles used in testing). In portrait orientation, as may occur with a phone-based camera, the largest face overall is selected, as spatial position is less reliable. All other detected faces are blurred using a Gaussian kernel sized proportionally to the face bounding box, ensuring sufficient obscuration regardless of distance from the camera. The blurred frames are written as the output video, so the original unblurred footage is never stored on the server. Gaussian blurring was chosen over pixelation because it is less visually jarring while still being irreversible, and over full-frame blurring because retaining the driver’s face allows manual verification of gaze classifications during evaluation.

During user testing on mobile devices, it was observed that loading the full-resolution output videos ($1920\times1080$) over some Wi-Fi networks or mobile data sources caused significant buffering. Since two concurrent video streams double the bandwidth requirement compared to a single-video application, adaptive quality selection was particularly important. To address this, the system generates multiple quality variants ($1280\times720$ and $854\times480$) of both output videos after analysis completes, scaling only the vertical dimension and preserving the original aspect ratio so that footage from non-standard sources (such as phones recording in portrait orientation) is handled correctly. The frontend estimates the user’s effective bandwidth and automatically selects an appropriate resolution, with the option to manually choose their resolution.

\section{Evaluation and Results}
Project evaluation was split into two parts: a quantitative evaluation against author-labelled ground truth, and a qualitative evaluation through user testing and a general interest questionnaire.

\subsection{Detection Accuracy and Literature Comparison}
The evaluation dataset comprises clips recorded both at day and at night (with an infra-red internal camera for night-time cabin recording). These labelled clips contain 104 manoeuvre events in total: 30 left turns, 24 right turns, 25 left lane changes and 25 right lane changes. The event types are approximately balanced, which was a deliberate choice during labelling to avoid biasing the evaluation towards any single manoeuvre category.

To evaluate the system’s event detection accuracy, a custom evaluation framework was developed. For each labelled video, the analysis pipeline was run on a window surrounding each ground truth event (30 seconds either side), and the resulting detections were compared against the labels using greedy temporal matching with a 4-second tolerance, consistent with the method that Zekany et al. used, discussed in Section 4.5. In this approach, ground truth events and detections are paired one-to-one by iteratively selecting the closest unmatched pair in time, ensuring that each ground truth event is matched to at most one detection and vice versa.

Unmatched ground truth events are counted as false negatives (FN, missed detections), and unmatched system detections are counted as false positives (FP, spurious detections). Matched pairs are true positives (TP). From these counts, three standard metrics are computed: precision (the proportion of detections that are real), recall
(the proportion of real events detected), and their harmonic mean $F_1$.

\begin{align*}
\text{Precision} &= \frac{TP}{TP + FP} \\\\
\text{Recall}    &= \frac{TP}{TP + FN} \\\\
F_1              &= \frac{2 \cdot \text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\end{align*}
Across the 104 labelled ground truth events, the system produced 103 detections. Greedy temporal matching yielded 84 matched detections, 19 false positives and 20 false negatives, giving an overall precision of 0.82, recall of 0.81 and F1 score of 0.81. The mean temporal error between matched ground truth and detection timestamps was 1.3 seconds. The closest comparable system in the literature is that of Zekany et al.\ \cite{zekany2022finding}. Table \ref{tab:f1_comparison} presents a direct per-manoeuvre comparison by taking the best (maximum F1) operating point for each of their individual classifiers from their Table III. It should be noted that the two systems were evaluated on entirely different datasets, though the evaluation procedure of Zekany et al. was followed as closely as possible.

In both systems, a detection is considered a true positive if it falls within the temporal tolerance of a ground truth event of the same manoeuvre category, so both precision and recall reflect correct type identification.

Our system achieves a higher F1 score on three of the four manoeuvre types. Lane changes were detected most reliably, with right lane changes achieving 96\% recall and 96\% precision; both lane change F1 scores more than double those of Zekany et al. Left turns also showed a substantial improvement (0.79 vs.\ 0.61). The only exception is right turns, where our system achieves an F1 of only 0.64 compared to their 0.89. This is primarily due to a high false positive rate for right turns, discussed below, which lowers precision to 0.59. Our lower recall for turns more generally (77\% for left, 71\% for right) is caused by our VO pipeline's weakness to externally moving objects; for example at junctions with many cars blocking majority of the scene, the segmentation mask results in a weaker turn signature when the vehicle makes a manoeuvre.

Of the 19 false positives, 12 were classified as right turns. The majority occurred on roads that curve rightward, producing a yaw accumulation pattern indistinguishable from a genuine right turn as far as the visual odometry is concerned. Since the pipeline relies solely on camera motion, it has no way to differentiate between a road that bends to the right and a deliberate right turn at a junction. Mitigating this would require incorporating additional environmental context, such as detecting give-way lines, junction markings, or changes in road topology that distinguish intersections from curved road segments (Figure \ref{fig:right_turn_misclass}). The remaining false positives for lane changes (6 of the 19 total) were predominantly caused by the lane detection model misclassifying bus lanes or faded road markings as lane boundaries. Zekany et al. acknowledge that they also observe the same susceptibility to road curves producing false turn detections.

Our system achieves higher classification accuracy on two of the four shared manoeuvre types: right turns (94\% vs.\ 80\%) and left lane changes (95\% vs.\ 7\%). Right lane change accuracy is comparable (96\% vs.\ 98\%), while Zekany et al.\ achieve higher accuracy on left turns (89\% vs.\ 83\%). Zekany et al.\ acknowledge that left and right lane changes ``alias frequently'' in their system, shown by 70\% of left lane changes being misclassified as right lane changes in their confusion matrix. Our system avoids this directional confusion almost entirely, because lane change direction is determined by explicit lane boundary crossing detection rather than trajectory shape matching.

\subsection{User Testing}
For user testing, participants were recruited from the author's personal network, including friends and family. All participants were fully qualified drivers with valid UK driving licences. They were asked to record their regular driving using their existing dash-cam alongside a phone mounted on the dashboard for the cabin-facing feed, or a dual-channel dash-cam system if available. No special driving instructions were given; participants simply drove as they normally would to ensure the footage reflected natural driving behaviour. After uploading and reviewing their results, participants could report any errors they identified through the built-in error reporting mechanism.

\subsubsection{User-Submitted Video Results}
Over a four-week period (early March to early April 2026), six external users registered accounts and submitted driving footage through the web application. In total, 23 unique video pairs (road-facing and driver-facing) were uploaded and successfully analysed, representing approximately 148 minutes of driving footage across a range of conditions and locations. Several users re-analysed the same footage after creating or refining their gaze calibration profiles; after deduplication (retaining the most recent calibrated analysis per unique video pair), the 23 analyses produced 170 safety events. Of the 23 unique analyses, 13 (57\%) used a personalised gaze calibration, while the remaining 10 relied on the system's default classifier.

Several users re-analysed their footage after creating or refining a personalised gaze calibration profile, providing a direct before-and-after comparison of calibration's effect on safety classifications. \subsubsection{User Error Reports}
As described in Section \ref{sec:eval_infra}, the web application includes an error reporting mechanism that allows users to flag false positive detections (events the system detected that did not occur) and false negative detections (real events the system missed). During the deployment, users submitted 13 error reports: 9 false positives and 4 false negatives.

The false positive reports were mainly right turn detections. Manual review through the administrator dashboard confirmed that these false positives were caused by winding roads with successive right-hand curves, producing visual odometry signals indistinguishable from genuine turns. This is consistent with the curved-road false positive pattern identified in the author-labelled evaluation (Section 5.1).

The 4 false negative reports identified two unique failure modes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Missing lane markings}: two users noted that right lane changes were missed because the road lacked visible lane markings, preventing the lane detection model from identifying a boundary crossing.
    \item \textbf{Direction misclassification}: one user reported that a left turn was classified as a right turn.
\end{enumerate}

These error reports show that the system would benefit from using other sources of information to identify manoeuvres in order to improve its accuracy. Across the 170 detected events, the 9 user-reported false positives imply a lower bound on the false positive rate of 5.3\% from the user's perspective, though this likely underestimates the true rate since users may not have flagged every incorrect detection. Conversely, the remaining 161 events (94.7\%) were accepted as accurate by users, meaning no disputes were raised about their detection or safety classification. All 137 safe classifications (100\%) were accepted, while 24 of the 33 unsafe classifications (72.7\%) were not disputed. The 24 undisputed unsafe events represent genuine mirror-check failures that the drivers themselves had not been aware of during their journeys, demonstrating the system's ability to surface mistakes that would otherwise go unnoticed. One user reported that after reviewing their results and seeing specific locations where they had failed to check their mirrors, they drove the same route again and found that they made fewer mistakes the second time, having been made aware of the habitual gaps in their mirror-checking behaviour. While this is a single anecdotal case, it provides early evidence that the system can fulfil its intended purpose as a learning tool.

Returning to the research question posed in the Introduction, ``Can a multi-modal system combining road-facing and driver-facing video detect driving mistakes at an accuracy of at least 80\%?'', the user testing results provide strong supporting evidence. The 94.7\% user-accepted accuracy across all events exceeds the 80\% target, and the controlled evaluation's F1 score of 0.81 independently corroborates this. While the 72.7\% acceptance rate for unsafe classifications falls below 80\%, this is largely attributable to the curved-road false positive pattern identified in the controlled evaluation, a known limitation with a clear path to resolution through additional environmental context.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
This section discusses the system's limitations, identifies directions for future development, and reflects on the project's overall contribution.

\subsection{Limitations}
Despite the strong evaluation results, the system has clear limitations. The most significant is the visual odometry pipeline's inability to distinguish road curvature from genuine turns, which accounted for the majority of false positives in both the controlled evaluation and user-reported errors. The lane detection model is also susceptible to UK-specific road markings such as bus lanes and faded markings, and roundabouts remain entirely unsupported due to the absence of suitable training data.

Beyond these technical limitations, the system faces a practical barrier to adoption. The multi-step workflow of recording on two devices, downloading, uploading, and calibrating proved friction-heavy during user testing, and low dash-cam ownership among contacted users further limited participation. Hardware integration with dual-channel dash-cam systems \cite{viofo_a229plus} or factory-fitted interior cameras \cite{jlr_dms_news} would reduce this friction considerably. The alternative applications outlined in the Introduction (insurance analytics, in-car alerts, ADAS training data annotation) may also represent more practical deployment paths, since dual-camera hardware is already present in those contexts.

\subsection{Future Work}
Many of these limitations have clear paths to resolution. The calibration burden could be reduced by combining the existing supervised approach with unsupervised clustering, since k-means already gave a close approximation to the desired result. Alternatively, real-time audio cues could guide drivers through a brief calibration routine during driving, collecting labelled samples without requiring a separate post-hoc step.

For detection accuracy, reducing false turn detections on curved roads is the most impactful improvement. Incorporating map data, GPS traces, or training a classifier on visual junction features (give-way lines, road widening, traffic light presence) could provide the necessary environmental context. Sensor fusion with smartphone IMU data would complement the vision-based estimate, mirroring the approach taken by ZenRoad \cite{zenroad_app}. Lane change detection could be improved by fine-tuning the lane detection model on UK-specific road markings.

The modular architecture also lends itself to detecting additional mistake types. Failure to indicate could be detected via an audio classifier trained on indicator sounds captured by the in-cabin microphone. Speed violations could be inferred by combining visual odometry velocity estimates with map data or smartphone accelerometers. Following-distance violations could be estimated by tracking the vehicle ahead from the dash-cam.

\subsection{Conclusions}
Returning to the research question posed in the Introduction, the results from both the controlled evaluation and the user study confirm that a multi-modal system combining road-facing and driver-facing video can reliably detect driving mistakes, exceeding the target accuracy. The favourable comparison against Zekany et al., particularly for lane changes, demonstrates that an explicit, rule-based manoeuvre detection approach can match or exceed existing methods while remaining fully interpretable.

What proved most valuable, however, was the live deployment. The vast majority of detected events were accepted as accurate by users, and the system surfaced genuine mirror-check failures that drivers had not been aware of during their journeys. This is precisely the value the system was designed to deliver, and it validates the core hypothesis that synchronising driver state with vehicle movement can provide insights unachievable by either modality alone. The iterative improvements driven by user feedback, especially reducing the calibration workflow from several minutes to under a minute, reinforced that usability is as important as detection accuracy in determining whether a system like this can be adopted in practice.

The positive reception from questionnaire respondents (74\% scoring likelihood of use at 6 or above, with specific feature requests such as mock driving test scoring and speed analysis) and the engagement of returning users suggest genuine demand for this type of tool. While the limitations identified above are real, they are well-characterised and have clear paths to resolution. The modular architecture provides a solid foundation for the extensions outlined in future work, and the project as a whole demonstrates that this multi-modal approach to driving mistake identification is both technically viable and practically valuable.
